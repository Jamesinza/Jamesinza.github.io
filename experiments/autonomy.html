Autonomy

Exploring what happens when learning systems make decisions about themselves.

Intent

This experiment began with the goal of building an autonomous learning system. In practice, the work shifted toward understanding why autonomy is hard and where current learning paradigms break down.

What Autonomy Means Here

Autonomy is not defined as goal-setting or agency in a philosophical sense. Instead, it refers to a system’s ability to:

  • adapt its learning strategy over time
  • alter internal decision pathways
  • respond to uncertainty without explicit supervision

Key Observations

  • increased flexibility often introduces instability
  • credit assignment becomes delayed and noisy
  • failure modes are harder to detect early

Many behaviors that look like “learning” are actually brittle equilibria.

Outcome

The system did not converge to clean autonomy and that is the result. The experiment exposed the hidden costs of adaptive control and clarified which mechanisms require tighter constraints.

Status

Paused. Insights from this work now inform later experiments focused on stability, structure, and bounded adaptivity.
